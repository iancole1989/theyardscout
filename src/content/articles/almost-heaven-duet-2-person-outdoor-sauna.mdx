## What the Almost Heaven Duet actually is

The Duet is Almost Heaven's smallest outdoor barrel sauna — a traditional Finnish-style sauna using a wet/dry heater (you can pour water on the rocks for steam, or run it dry). It sits at the entry tier of Almost Heaven's outdoor lineup, below the Salem (4-person), the Audra (4-person variant), and the larger cabin-style saunas.

Position in the Almost Heaven lineup:

- **Duet (this model)**: 2-person barrel, ~$3,000-3,800 — entry tier
- **Salem / Pinnacle**: 4-person barrel, ~$4,500-5,500 — mid tier
- **Audra / Allegro**: 4-person variants with different bench configurations, ~$5,000-6,500
- **Bridgeport / Princeton**: 6+ person cabin saunas, $7,000-12,000 — premium tier

The Duet specifically targets buyers who want authentic traditional sauna in the smallest possible footprint. It's not a budget cut of the larger saunas — it's the same cedar grade, same heater family, same construction method, just sized for 2 people.

## Cedar quality reality — the part that matters most

Cedar grade is the variable that most affects real-world ownership of any barrel sauna. Almost Heaven publishes that the Duet uses Canadian red cedar — true. What's less publicized: it's **rustic grade**, which means:

- **Knots are permitted** (clear grade has none)
- **Tight knots stay tight; loose knots may pop** over the first 1-2 heating cycles, leaving small holes (cosmetic, not structural)
- **Color variation is significant** — boards range from pale honey to deep red-brown within the same sauna
- **Small splits and checking are expected** as the wood acclimates to heat/humidity cycling
- **Sap pockets** are possible (small resin spots that may weep slightly when heated)

**This is not a quality defect** — rustic-grade cedar at this price point is industry-standard. It's also what most owners are actually buying when they pay $3,000-3,800. Clear-grade cedar at the same dimensions would be $5,500-6,500+, and the practical difference is purely aesthetic.

Where rustic-grade cedar matters:
- **First-impression aesthetics**: clear cedar looks like a magazine photo; rustic cedar looks like a working sauna
- **First-year maintenance**: rustic grade may need 1-2 small interior touch-ups in year one (sanding a knot that popped, sealing a small sap spot)
- **Long-term durability**: identical to clear grade — cedar is cedar regardless of knot count for purposes of heat/humidity performance

If you're paying full price expecting magazine-perfect aesthetics, you'll be disappointed. If you understand you're buying a working sauna with the natural variation of real wood, the Duet's cedar is genuinely good for the money.

## Heater options and electrical requirements

This is the single biggest install decision and the most common owner-regret point.

### Electric heater (3.5kW Harvia) — the default

The Duet ships standard with a 3.5kW Harvia electric heater. Specs:

- **Voltage**: 240V single-phase
- **Current**: ~15A continuous, 20A circuit recommended
- **Heat-up time**: ~25-35 minutes to operating temp (160-180°F) from cold
- **Rock capacity**: ~30 lbs of sauna stones (steam generation)

The 240V requirement is non-negotiable for the electric heater. If your property doesn't have an existing 240V outlet within range of the sauna location, you're hiring an electrician. Expect $400-1,500 for a typical install depending on distance to the panel, conduit type, and local labor rates. This cost is **not** included in the sauna price and is often the surprise.

**Wiring details to confirm with your electrician:**
- 10-gauge wire minimum for a 20A circuit at typical distances
- GFCI protection is generally required by code for outdoor installations
- Outdoor-rated conduit (PVC schedule 40 or metal)
- The heater connects via a dedicated junction box, not a plug-and-receptacle setup
- Permit may be required (depends on jurisdiction)

### Wood-fired heater (Harvia M3) — the alternative

The wood-fired option replaces the electric heater with a Harvia M3 wood-burning stove. Trade-offs:

- **No electrical work needed** — saves $400-1,500 and any permitting headache
- **Real fire experience** — the smell, the crackling, the heat character is different (and many traditional sauna enthusiasts strongly prefer it)
- **Slower heat-up** — 45-60 minutes from cold start vs 25-35 for electric
- **Requires chimney** — the M3 vents through a stainless steel chimney pipe; that's an additional $200-400 component
- **Requires fuel storage** — you need a wood supply and somewhere to store it dry
- **Requires active management** — feeding the stove every 15-30 minutes during use

The wood-fired path is the more authentic Finnish experience but adds operator labor every session. Many electric-heater owners look back and wish they'd gone wood-fired; some wood-fired owners look back and wish they had electric. There's no objectively right answer.

## Install reality

Beyond electrical, the practical install considerations:

**Foundation requirements.** The Duet weighs ~600-700 lbs assembled. You need:
- A flat, level surface (a 1° slope is enough to misalign the door over time)
- Adequate load-bearing capacity
- Drainage if rain or snow accumulates around it
- Options: concrete pad ($300-800 if DIY, $1,000-2,500 if poured pro), gravel pad on landscape fabric ($150-400), or pressure-treated wood deck rated for the load

**Site clearance.** The barrel itself is ~4 ft diameter x 6 ft long. You also need:
- 24+ inches of door swing clearance in front
- 12+ inches of clearance on all sides for maintenance access
- Roof overhang clearance if installing under a roof structure
- Total useful footprint: ~6 ft x 8 ft minimum

**Assembly.** The Duet ships flat-packed and requires owner assembly. With 2 people, plan 4-6 hours. The bands that hold the barrel staves together are the most fiddly part — they need to be tightened progressively rather than fully on one side at a time. Almost Heaven's instructions are adequate; YouTube walkthroughs from other owners help with the bands specifically.

**Roof choice.** Three options:
- **Asphalt shingle** (standard): cheapest, adequate, looks utilitarian
- **Cedar shingle**: $200-400 upgrade, looks dramatically better, weathers gracefully
- **Rubber/EPDM roof**: most weatherproof, looks industrial, $300-500 upgrade

If aesthetics matter and budget allows, cedar shingles are the operator pick. If you're in a sustained-wet climate (PNW, coastal NE), the rubber roof is more practical despite the look.

## Performance reality

### Heat-up time and operating temp

- **From cold to operating temp (160-180°F)**: 25-35 minutes (electric); 45-60 minutes (wood-fired)
- **Steady-state heat hold**: heater cycles on/off to maintain set temp
- **Steam generation**: pour water on rocks for steam bursts; ~1 cup of water per steam pour for a noticeable effect

The 3.5kW heater is matched to the small interior — it heats up reasonably fast and holds temperature well. Larger Almost Heaven saunas with 6kW+ heaters take longer to heat but hold more thermal mass.

### Actual session experience

A typical 30-minute sauna session in the Duet:
- 25-35 min preheat (you can start preheat from inside your house with the included timer/control panel — check current model features)
- 8-12 min of dry heat (160-180°F)
- 1-2 min cooldown / steam pour for löyly experience
- 8-12 min of warmer humid heat (180-200°F effective with steam)
- 2-3 min exit and cooldown

Two adults in the Duet feels comfortable but cozy. The single-bench layout means both people are at the same elevation — you don't get the option to sit lower (cooler) or higher (hotter) that two-tier benches in larger saunas provide.

### Energy cost

**Electric heater operation cost:**
- ~3.5 kWh per hour of operation
- Typical 30-min session = ~1.75 kWh = $0.20-0.45 per session at $0.12-0.25/kWh
- Heavier 60-min session = ~3.5 kWh = $0.40-0.90 per session

**Monthly cost at 4 sessions/week**: $4-15 (electric). Wood-fired cost depends on wood cost in your area but typically lower at the per-session level if you source cheap wood.

This is dramatically cheaper to operate than a hot tub. Saunas are heated only when in use; hot tubs hold heat 24/7. Different operating model.

## Ownership reality

### Year 1: settling in

The first year is the wood-acclimation period. Expect:
- 1-2 small knot pops in the first few high-heat sessions (cosmetic patch)
- Minor checking (small surface splits) in some boards — normal for cedar
- Slight color darkening of the interior wood from heat exposure (this is the desired patina)
- The bands holding the barrel may need a small re-tightening after the first 3-6 months as wood settles

None of this is a defect. All of it is normal cedar barrel sauna behavior.

### Year 2-5: stable operation

Most Duet owners report effectively no issues during years 2-5 with normal use (3-5 sessions per week). The cedar darkens to a richer color, the bands stabilize, the heater operates reliably.

Maintenance during this period:
- Wipe down the interior bench surfaces monthly (mild soap; let dry)
- Sweep out leaves/debris from the floor 1-2x per month
- Check the door seal annually
- Sand minor rough spots if needed (rare)

### Year 5-10: heater service window

Harvia heaters are durable but not lifetime equipment. Around year 7-10 with normal use, expect to need:
- Heating element replacement (~$80-180 part + 30 minutes labor) — DIY-able for electrically comfortable owners
- Control board replacement if you have the digital model (~$200-300 part)
- Stone replacement (the rocks degrade with thermal cycling) — $30-60 every 3-5 years

For wood-fired (Harvia M3), expect:
- Gasket replacement every 3-5 years (~$30)
- Chimney pipe inspection annually
- Stone replacement same as electric

### Year 10+: cedar replacement consideration

After 10+ years of regular use, the interior cedar may need refinishing or partial replacement — particularly the bench surfaces which take direct body contact and moisture. This is more a refurbishment than a failure point. Many Duets are still operating after 15+ years with minor refinishing.

## Alternatives at adjacent price points

- **Cheaper / smaller — Generic 2-person barrel saunas** (~$1,800-2,500): brands like SaunaLife, Maxxus, and various Amazon-direct sellers. Construction quality and heater quality are noticeably below Almost Heaven but functional for occasional use.
- **Same tier, infrared instead — Sun Home Solstice or similar** (~$3,500-4,500): full-spectrum infrared, faster heat-up, different sweat experience (radiant vs ambient). See our [Sun Home Saunas review](/sun-home-saunas/).
- **Step up — Almost Heaven Salem or Pinnacle 4-person** (~$4,500-5,500): same construction quality, larger capacity, two-tier bench. Worth the price step if you have 3+ regular users.
- **Premium tier — Clearlight Infrared** (~$5,000-7,000): premium infrared brand with low-EMF technology. Different category — infrared not traditional. See our [Clearlight sauna review](/clearlight-infrared-sauna-price/).

## Bottom line

The Almost Heaven Duet is a legitimately good 2-person traditional sauna for buyers who specifically want that combination of size, traditional steam (not infrared), real cedar (not veneer), and US-manufactured quality. It's not premium aesthetics and it's not the cheapest 2-person barrel on the market — it's solid mid-tier construction at fair pricing.

The single most common regret: underestimating the 240V install cost. If you don't have 240V already wired to the sauna's location, factor $400-1,500 of additional electrical work or commit to the wood-fired option upfront. Either approach is fine — but plan for it before you order.

For 4+ regular users, step up to the Salem. For infrared sauna preference, look at Clearlight or Sun Home. For traditional steam in a 2-person form, the Duet is the operator pick at this price.
