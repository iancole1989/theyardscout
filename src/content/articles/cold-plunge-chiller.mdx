## How chillers work in a cold plunge setup

A water chiller is a refrigeration unit specifically designed to cool flowing water. The components:

- **Compressor**: The work-doing unit; this is what's measured in HP
- **Heat exchanger**: Where the refrigerant absorbs heat from the water
- **Refrigerant loop**: Sealed gas circuit that transfers heat from water to outside air
- **Fan**: Exhausts heat away from the chiller (chiller itself blows warm air during operation)

**How it integrates with your tub:**

1. Pump pulls water from the tub through a hose
2. Water flows through the chiller's heat exchanger (where it gets cooled)
3. Cooled water returns to the tub through another hose
4. Cycle continues until temperature controller reaches target setpoint
5. Chiller cycles on/off to maintain target temperature

**Critical:** the chiller itself sits OUTSIDE the water. Only the water flow loop touches the cold water. This is fundamentally safer than chest-freezer DIY approaches where the electrical heating element is in physical proximity to standing water.

## HP sizing — the most important decision

Undersizing is the #1 chiller mistake. Sizing depends on:

1. **Tank volume** (how much water to chill)
2. **Target temperature** (lower target = more work)
3. **Ambient temperature** (warmer air = harder for chiller to dump heat)
4. **Insulation** (better-insulated tank = less heat ingress = smaller chiller works)
5. **Usage pattern** (continuous holding vs reactive cooling after each use)

### Sizing chart

| Tank volume | Target temp | Ambient | Recommended HP |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 80-120 gal | 50°F+ | 70-80°F | 1/4 HP |
| 100-150 gal | 45-50°F | 70-80°F | 1/4 to 1/3 HP |
| 150-200 gal | 42-48°F | 70-85°F | 1/3 HP |
| 150-200 gal | 38-42°F | 70-85°F | 1/2 HP |
| 200-250 gal | 42-50°F | 80-95°F | 1/2 HP |
| 250+ gal | 38-45°F | 80-95°F | 3/4 to 1 HP |
| Any | 38-42°F sustained | 90°F+ (Arizona, Florida summer) | 1 HP |

**Rules of thumb:**

- For a typical 150-gallon stock tank holding 45°F: **1/3 HP is the safe pick**
- For a typical 100-gallon tub holding 50°F: 1/4 HP works
- For aggressive cold (38-42°F sustained): step up one HP class from above

**Don't undersize.** A 1/4 HP chiller fighting a 200-gallon tank at 40°F in Texas summer is going to run continuously and never reach target. You'll either accept warmer temperatures (60°F+) or buy a bigger chiller later.

## Top chiller brands for cold plunge

### Penguin Chillers (purpose-built for cold plunge)

**Position**: The brand most consciously designed for cold plunge DIY. Founded ~2020 specifically targeting the cold therapy market.

**Strengths:**
- Purpose-built for cold plunge use cases
- Temperature controller integrated (some models)
- Strong customer support for cold plunge specifically
- 2-year warranty standard
- US-based support

**Weaknesses:**
- 10-20% more expensive than equivalent aquaculture chillers
- Newer brand — less long-term reliability data
- Limited model range (mostly 1/4 to 1 HP)

**Pricing:**
- 1/4 HP: $600-900
- 1/3 HP: $800-1,100
- 1/2 HP: $1,000-1,400
- 1 HP: $1,500-2,000

**Best for:** First-time DIY cold plunge builders who want a brand that specifically supports the use case.

### Active Aqua (aquaculture / hydroponics)

**Position**: Industry-standard chiller for indoor hydroponics + aquaculture. Adapts well to cold plunge.

**Strengths:**
- Established brand with strong reliability data (used in commercial aquaculture)
- Wide model range from 1/10 HP to 5 HP
- Cheaper than Penguin at equivalent capacity (15-25% less)
- Replacement parts widely available

**Weaknesses:**
- Designed for continuous-flow cooling (aquariums); cycling on/off for cold plunge use is less ideal
- No purpose-fit accessories or controllers — DIY everything around the unit
- Customer support is hydroponics-focused, not cold-plunge-aware

**Pricing:**
- 1/4 HP: $400-650
- 1/3 HP: $550-800
- 1/2 HP: $750-1,100
- 1 HP: $1,200-1,700

**Best for:** Budget-conscious DIY builders who don't need a brand to hold their hand through cold-plunge specifics.

### EcoPlus (hydroponics)

**Position**: Similar to Active Aqua — hydroponics-aimed brand, works fine for cold plunge.

**Strengths:**
- Often cheapest equivalent capacity (10-20% below Active Aqua)
- Widely available on Amazon and hydroponics retailers
- Good reliability data from hydroponics community

**Weaknesses:**
- Lowest-tier of the three brands on customer support quality
- Slightly louder operation than Active Aqua at equivalent HP
- No cold-plunge-specific accessories

**Pricing:**
- 1/4 HP: $350-550
- 1/3 HP: $500-700
- 1/2 HP: $650-950
- 1 HP: $1,100-1,500

**Best for:** Maximum cost efficiency for DIY builders who are comfortable troubleshooting.

### Specialty / smaller brands

**Coolworks** — newer entrant with marine-grade chillers. Premium pricing ($1,200-2,500). Niche fit.

**JBJ Aquaria** — aquarium-tier chillers, smaller capacities (1/10 to 1/4 HP). Better for very small tanks.

**Avoid:** Generic Amazon chillers under $300. Quality is typically poor, lifespan 1-3 years, no meaningful warranty support.

## Beyond the chiller — what else you need

A chiller alone doesn't make a cold plunge. The full DIY setup includes:

### Circulation pump

The chiller doesn't have its own pump in most cases — you need a separate submersible or external pump to push water through the chiller's heat exchanger.

- **Submersible pump**: Sits in the tub, pushes water out through one port and back through another
- **External pump**: Sits outside the tub, plumbed in-line with the chiller
- **Flow rate needed**: Match the chiller's specified flow rate (typically 350-600 GPH for 1/4-1/3 HP chillers)

**Cost**: $50-150 for a quality pump (EcoPlus, Active Aqua, or Hydrofarm brands).

### Plumbing and fittings

- 1/2" or 3/4" reinforced PVC or vinyl hose (rated for cold + flowing water)
- Bulkhead fittings to enter/exit the tank cleanly
- Hose clamps
- Optional: in-line shutoff valves for easy chiller disconnection

**Cost**: $30-100 depending on complexity.

### Temperature controller (optional but recommended)

The chiller's built-in thermostat is often basic. An external Inkbird or similar temperature controller gives you:
- Precise temperature targets (within 0.5°F)
- Compressor protection (prevents short-cycling)
- Optional probe placement for accurate water temperature reading

**Cost**: $40-80 (Inkbird ITC-1000 or similar).

### GFCI outlet

Non-negotiable for any cold plunge setup with electrical components in proximity to water. Standard outlets are unacceptable — water + 110V appliance contact requires GFCI ground-fault protection.

**Cost**: $80-200 to install a new GFCI outlet if you don't have one.

### Tank / tub

The container itself.

- **Galvanized stock tank** (100-200 gallons): $200-400 at Tractor Supply, Rural King, farm supply stores
- **Premium plastic tub** (Plunge-style): $400-1,000 for unbranded equivalents on Amazon
- **Wood tub** (cedar): $800-2,500 for premium aesthetics

Most DIY builders use galvanized stock tanks for durability + cost.

## Operating cost reality

After the upfront install, ongoing cost is modest:

- **Electricity**: $15-40/month with chiller cycling to maintain temp (~1-2 kWh/day for 1/3 HP chiller)
- **Water chemistry**: $20-30/month for bromine sanitizer; $0 for weekly water changes
- **Filter replacement** (if using inline filter): $10-20/month

**Total monthly operational cost: $35-90.** Dramatically cheaper than commercial cold plunge subscription services or studio visits.

## Install gotchas

After tracking DIY cold plunge builds for a while, these are the recurring mistakes:

1. **Undersized chiller.** "I'll save $200 by going 1/4 HP for my 200 gallon tank." Result: chiller runs continuously, can't reach target temp, owner buys a bigger chiller 3 months later.
2. **Skipping the temperature controller.** Cycling on internal chiller thermostat alone produces wide temperature swings (±5°F). External controller cuts swings to ±1°F.
3. **Wrong pump capacity.** Pump moves water slower than chiller can cool, or faster than chiller can cool effectively. Match GPH to chiller spec.
4. **Indoor chiller install.** Chiller exhausts heat — running indoors in a small room heats the room while cooling the water. Outdoor or garage install solves this.
5. **No GFCI.** Single most dangerous oversight. Always GFCI.
6. **Chiller too close to tub.** Chiller fans exhaust warm air toward the tub, slightly raising water temp. Place chiller 3-5 ft away minimum.
7. **No cover on tub.** Open tubs accumulate heat from sun + ambient air. Cover when not in use reduces chiller workload 30-40%.

## Bottom line

For most DIY cold plunge builders:

- **Tank**: 150-gallon galvanized stock tank, $250-350
- **Chiller**: Penguin 1/3 HP or Active Aqua 1/3 HP, $550-900
- **Pump + plumbing + controller + GFCI**: $200-400
- **Total**: $1,000-1,650 all-in

This setup holds reliable 42-48°F water in temperate climates with modest insulation. Adequate for 95% of cold plunge use cases.

For aggressive cold targets (38-42°F sustained), warmer climates, or larger tubs, step up to 1/2 HP. For occasional / light use only, 1/4 HP saves $150-250 with the trade-off of warmer minimum temperatures.
