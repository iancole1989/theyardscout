## What infrared sauna actually is (vs traditional)

Quick distinction since this is where many first-time infrared users get confused:

- **Traditional sauna**: Heater + rocks, dry hot air at 160-200°F, optional steam from water on rocks. Heats the AIR around you.
- **Infrared sauna**: Radiant heat panels emitting infrared light, air temperature 110-140°F (much lower), radiant heat penetrates skin directly. Heats YOU, not the air.

The experience is different. Traditional sauna sessions are typically 8-15 minutes (the high ambient temperature limits session length). Infrared sessions are 30-45 minutes (lower ambient temp tolerated for longer). Some users strongly prefer one; others go back and forth.

**Far infrared vs full-spectrum:**
- **Far infrared (FIR)**: Older technology, longer wavelengths, primarily heats the body's surface
- **Mid infrared (MIR)**: Deeper penetration
- **Near infrared (NIR)**: Shortest wavelengths, claimed deepest penetration (research is mixed)
- **Full-spectrum**: Combines all three. Most premium studios + premium home saunas use full-spectrum.

A "full-spectrum" panel produces the broadest infrared experience. Studios that only offer far-infrared are typically running older equipment.

## How to evaluate a studio before booking

Don't trust the studio's own marketing claims. Evaluate based on these operator-level criteria:

### Equipment quality

**Ask the studio:**
- What brand of infrared panels do you use? (Look for: Clearlight, Sunlighten, Higher Dose Infrared, Sun Home, JNH — recognizable premium brands)
- Full-spectrum or far-infrared only?
- What's the EMF rating? (Better studios know this; mark it as a green flag when they answer confidently)

**EMF (electromagnetic field) levels** matter because you're sitting in close proximity to electrical heating elements for 30-45 minutes. Premium infrared brands engineer for low EMF — typically below 3 milligauss (mG) at the bench surface. Studios using generic or older equipment may have EMF levels 10-50× higher.

### Hygiene and setup

- **Private vs shared room**: Private is dramatically better — privacy, hygiene, and ability to fully undress for maximum benefit
- **Towels provided**: Should include a body towel + face towel minimum
- **Water provided**: Hydration before/during/after is critical for infrared sessions
- **Shower available**: Bonus, especially for post-session
- **Cleaning protocol**: Ask how often benches are sanitized; should be after every session

### Session structure

- **Minimum 30 minutes**: Shorter sessions don't deliver the temperature ramp infrared needs
- **Pre-heating policy**: Quality studios pre-heat the cabin 15-20 min before your session
- **Heat range adjustment**: Can you control the temperature during your session?
- **Music/lighting options**: Quality of life; minor but indicates studio attention to experience

### Pricing structure

- **Single session**: $30-75 typical range
- **5-pack**: 10-15% discount per session
- **10-pack**: 15-25% discount per session
- **Monthly unlimited**: $80-200/month at most studios; only worth it if you'll genuinely go 8+ times/month
- **Membership commitments**: Read the contract carefully — some studios require 3-6 month minimum commitments

**Watch out for:** "$15 trial sessions" that auto-enroll you in monthly membership. These are membership-acquisition funnels, not honest pricing. The genuine first-session discount most quality studios offer is 30-50% off (e.g., $25-40 for a first-time session), not $15.

## Finding studios in your area

Practical search approach:

1. **Google Maps** for "infrared sauna" + your city. Sort by rating, read reviews for 3-5 candidates.
2. **ClassPass** if you have it — many infrared studios participate, gives you a low-commitment way to try multiple studios.
3. **MindBody app** — most fitness/wellness studios book through it.
4. **Higher Dose locations** if you're in NYC, LA, SF — they're a premium chain with consistent quality.
5. **Local gyms** — some include infrared sauna access in higher-tier memberships.
6. **Crossfit gyms / recovery studios** — often have infrared as a recovery amenity.

When reading reviews, weigh recent reviews more than old ones (equipment may have been replaced; management may have changed). Look for reviewer complaints about cleanliness, EMF concerns, pushy sales tactics, or membership lock-ins.

## Studio session — what to expect

If you've never done infrared, the experience differs from a gym sauna:

**Pre-session:** Drink 16-20 oz of water in the hour before. Don't eat heavily within 2 hours. Wear minimal/loose clothing (or nude is fine in a private room).

**During session:** Temperature ramps from ambient to 130-140°F over the first 10-15 minutes. You'll start sweating around minute 15-20 — much later than a traditional sauna, which sweats you in 5-7 minutes. Stay 30-40 minutes total.

**Post-session:** Cool down for 5-10 minutes before standing. Rehydrate aggressively (another 16-20 oz minimum). Cool/lukewarm shower; avoid hot showers immediately after as they prolong the elevated heart rate.

**Frequency:** 2-4 sessions per week is the typical "regular use" pattern. Daily is fine for healthy adults but plateau effects suggest 3 quality sessions beat 7 minimal sessions.

## Buy vs continue studio visits — the actual math

This is where the operator-honest answer matters most.

### Cost of studio visits over 5 years

| Pattern | Per session | Sessions/year | 5-year cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Light (1×/week) | $50 | 52 | $13,000 |
| Regular (2×/week) | $50 | 104 | $26,000 |
| Heavy (3×/week) | $50 | 156 | $39,000 |
| Daily | $50 | 312 | $78,000 |

With monthly unlimited memberships at $130/month, 5-year cost is $7,800 — better if you actually go 3+ times/week.

### Cost of owning a home infrared sauna

| Tier | Brand examples | Upfront | 5-year operating | 5-year total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Budget | Generic / smaller brands | $1,500-2,500 | $300-600 | $1,800-3,100 |
| Mid | Sun Home Solstice, JNH | $3,000-4,500 | $400-800 | $3,400-5,300 |
| Premium | Clearlight, Sunlighten | $5,000-8,000 | $500-1,000 | $5,500-9,000 |

**Break-even analysis:**
- Budget home sauna ($2,000) vs $50 studio sessions: break-even at 40 sessions = 10 months of regular use
- Mid-tier home sauna ($4,000) vs $50 studio sessions: 80 sessions = 13-14 months
- Premium home sauna ($6,000) vs $50 studio sessions: 120 sessions = 18-20 months

After break-even, ALL incremental sessions are essentially free (just electricity at ~$0.50/session). Studio visits cost the same per session forever.

**For anyone planning regular long-term use (2+ years), buying makes financial sense.** For occasional use or short commitment, studios make sense.

### Where studio visits stay better than buying

- You travel frequently and can't take a sauna with you (studio = different cities, different studios)
- You're testing whether infrared is for you (do 5-10 sessions first, then decide)
- You don't have install space (apartment, condo, small house)
- Friends/social use case where the studio social element matters
- Equipment maintenance is a deal-breaker for you

### Where buying wins

- Steady 3+ sessions per week for 12+ months
- Privacy/comfort preference for home use
- Want unlimited duration (studios cap at 45-60 min; home is unlimited)
- Premium home sauna build quality matters to you long-term
- You want to use the sauna at off-hours studios don't offer (5am, 11pm)

## Bottom line

If you're new to infrared, do 5-10 studio sessions to confirm the experience works for you. The sweat profile is different from traditional sauna and not everyone prefers it. Test before you buy.

If you're already a regular and going 3+ times per week, run the math — buying a home infrared sauna typically pays back within 10-20 months of regular use. After payback, every session is essentially free. Studio visits remain useful for travel, social use, and equipment-failure backup, but the financial math at regular cadence favors ownership.
